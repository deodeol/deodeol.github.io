It begins with a preamble that states the reasons for declaring independence, including the colonists' belief in natural rights and their grievances against King George III and his administration.

The main body of the Declaration of Independence consists of a list of grievances against the British government, including taxation without representation, the quartering of British soldiers in colonial homes, and the suppression of colonial trade. It also includes a statement of the colonists' belief in natural rights, such as life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness.

After listing the grievances, the Declaration of Independence concludes with a formal declaration of independence, stating that the colonies are "free and independent states" and have the right to "do all acts and things which independent states may of right do." It is signed by fifty-six representatives of the thirteen colonies.
